## Root Cause

Open-ended feedback loops + ambiguous decision criteria = slow approval cycles.

### The Anti-Pattern
**"What do you think of Phase 1?"**
- Open-ended question
- No explicit approval criteria
- Receiver must form complete mental model before responding
- Response cycle: 3–7 days (integrating with other priorities)

### The Better Pattern
**"Phase 1 is ready. Choose: (A) Approve + launch Phase 2 today [→ ships March 17–18] or (B) Request specific revisions [→ ships March 24+]. If B, list exactly which items. Response needed by 5 PM EST Friday."**
- Binary choice (reduces cognitive load)
- Explicit approval criteria (clear what "good" means)
- Hard deadline (forces priority)
- Decision cycle: 4–8 hours (often <1 hour)

**Effect:** 80% reduction in approval time (7 days → 4–8 hours)

## The Framework: Binary Decisions + Hard Deadlines

### Pattern
**Whenever a decision is needed, frame it as a binary choice with hard deadline and explicit criteria.**

1. **State the options clearly** ("A means X, B means Y")
2. **Specify the consequence of each** ("A → delivers March 17; B → delivers March 24")
3. **Name the decision criteria** ("If A: approve Phase 1. If B: list exact revisions needed.")
4. **Set a hard deadline** ("Response by 5 PM EST Friday")
5. **Be specific about what "good" means** (Avoid: "What do you think?" Prefer: "Pick A or B")

### Time Savings
- Standard process: 7+ days (open feedback loop)
- Binary frame: 4–8 hours (bounded decision)
- **Net gain:** 90%+ reduction in approval time

## When This Works Best

✅ **Binary decisions excel when:**
- High stakes (e.g., launch timing, resource allocation)
- Clear trade-offs between options (A vs B, not ambiguous)
- Information is already available (not waiting for research)
- Time sensitivity exists (decision delay costs something)

❌ **Binary decisions don't work when:**
- Information is missing (need research before choosing)
- Options are not clear (need exploration, not binary choice)
- Decision criteria are undefined (need to agree on what matters first)
